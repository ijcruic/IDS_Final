About This Method

The fundamental theory behind this analysis that individual's behavior, expecially shared beahviour with other indivdiuals, leads to the formation of social groups. In this case, we are taking voting behavior as the driving behavior for the formation of political groups within a political body. This approach is not a new one. There have been methods employed to find facitons through voting behavior since the 1920's. Most of the work on faction detection through roll call votes has focused on American institutions like the U.S. Congress or the U.S. Supreme Court. Also, most of the methods employed today rely on either estimating voters 'ideal points' in a latent space using probabilistic utility functions fit on their voting behavior, or Guttman-style scaling methods. All of these methods require a binary outcome for a vote ( i.e. you can only vote 'for' or 'against' something). The methods on this page, while from a less popular strain of research, allow a researcher to overcome the binary votes and independent voting assumptions. Moreover, the Gower's Method also allows for a researcher to inject subject matter expertise or test theories about the voting behavior through specially-crafted weighting functions.

About This Data

This data set consists of the roll call vote faction detection results for all proposed votes on the floor of the Ukrainian Parliament. Our Research Group has also enriched this data by adding in attributes about the Ukrainian Parliamentarians (i.e. age, method of election, profession, etc.).

  • Access to this data: The data is currently proprietary, since it is still being completed and is part of a major research project. So, the data is only available for access and use from the CASOS research group.
  • Accountability of this data: Since the actualy roll call voting results come directly from the official, digital records of the Ukrainian palriament, the data can be traced to the source and has regulations guiding its correct reporting. As for the attributes, since these have been colelcted from various sources, some official like the Ukrainian Parliament's website and some unofficial like public facing blogs or facebook pages, the data is not neecesarily strongly ties to the underlying phenomenon and could be subject to change at different measurement intervals.
  • Coherence of data: There have been no identified coherence issues with the roll call data. The distributions of vote repsonses matches what our subject matter experts have told us to expect (i.e. mostly 'yay' votes, with a good number of 'abstain' votes and very few 'nay' votes). Initial User Feedback

The ability to plot spatial positions of individuals by some shared characteristic or beahviour has always been desirable to social scientists. For this visualization I first experiemnted with 2-dimensional and 3-diemnsional plots. After showing both to some fellow researchers, who are working on the same question, we came to the conclusion that additional information is gained from 3-d dimensional plots, and so we discarded this idea and stuck with only 2 latent dimensions for representing the parliamentarians. Outside of that, fellow researchers like the ability to switch between different groupings and convocations. The only thing that was still desired that we were not able to implement was to display some kind of numerical measure of differences between different coefficient of similairites, or grouping algorithms, (like an adjusted rand index), next to the visual displays to get a better understanding of the differences between methods. I have also recieved feedback to include some different coloring methods, such as Louvain clustering.
